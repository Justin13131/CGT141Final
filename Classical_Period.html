Classical

Symphony No. 40 in G Minor – Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart

Composed in 1788, this is one of only two symphonies Mozart wrote in a minor key. It’s renowned for its emotional intensity and dramatic urgency, which was rare for symphonic music at the time. The first movement’s stormy character reflects a shift from the light elegance of early Classical style toward more expressive Romanticism. Mozart never heard this performed publicly in his lifetime.

Symphony No. 5 in C Minor – Ludwig van Beethoven

Completed in 1808, Beethoven’s Fifth Symphony is arguably the most famous symphony ever written. The iconic four-note motif — "da-da-da-DUM" — is recognized worldwide. It reflects Beethoven's struggle with fate and impending deafness. The symphony revolutionized Classical form by introducing deeper emotion, thematic development, and a dramatic journey from darkness to triumph.

Franz Joseph Haydn – Symphony No. 94 “Surprise” (2nd Movement)

Nicknamed the “Surprise Symphony”, Haydn composed this in 1791 during his first trip to London. The second movement starts softly and then—BANG!—delivers a loud unexpected chord to jolt the audience awake. Haydn, the “Father of the Symphony,” used humor and wit to make Classical music more engaging, and this is his most famous example.
